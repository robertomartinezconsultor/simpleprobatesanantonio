Advanced Estate Planning

Skip Probate Entirely. Protect Your Privacy.

Probate is the court process that validates a will and distributes your property after you pass away. In Texas, even a straightforward probate can take months, cost thousands of dollars in legal fees, and turn your private family matters into a public record that anyone can access. A revocable living trust lets your family skip all of that.

When you create a living trust, you transfer your property into the trust during your lifetime. You remain in complete control -- you can buy, sell, change, or revoke anything at any time. You are the trustee. Nothing changes about how you live your daily life. But when you pass away, your successor trustee (the person you choose) can distribute your assets to your beneficiaries without ever stepping foot in a courtroom.

A living trust is not just for the wealthy. It is for anyone who values privacy, wants to spare their family from probate, or has property in multiple states. It is also one of the best tools for families dealing with blended families, special needs beneficiaries, or concerns about creditors.

Why Privacy Matters

When a will goes through probate, it becomes a public document. That means anyone -- neighbors, strangers, solicitors -- can look up your will and see who inherited what, how much your estate was worth, and where your beneficiaries live. A living trust is a private document. It never gets filed with the court, and its contents remain confidential. For many families in San Antonio, that peace of mind is priceless.

Built-In Incapacity Protection

One of the most overlooked benefits of a living trust is what happens if you become incapacitated. If you have a trust, your successor trustee can step in immediately to manage your finances and assets without going to court. Without a trust, your family may need to pursue a guardianship -- a costly, time-consuming, and emotionally draining court process.

Is This Right for You?

Who Needs a Living Trust?

Privacy-Conscious Families

If you do not want your financial details, beneficiary names, and asset values to become public record, a trust keeps everything confidential.

Property Owners in Multiple States

Without a trust, your family may need to go through probate in every state where you own property. A trust consolidates everything and avoids multiple probate proceedings.

Blended Families

A trust gives you precise control over who receives what, when they receive it, and under what conditions -- reducing the risk of disputes between stepchildren and spouses.

Families with Special Needs Members

A special needs sub-trust can provide for a disabled family member without disqualifying them from government benefits like SSI or Medicaid.

Older Adults Concerned About Incapacity

If you are worried about who will manage your finances if you develop dementia or suffer a debilitating illness, a trust provides a seamless transition without court involvement.

Anyone Who Wants to Spare Their Family

Probate is stressful, slow, and expensive. If you want your family to grieve in peace rather than deal with courts, a trust is the most compassionate gift you can give.
