How to Probate a Will in San Antonio, Texas — Step by Step (2026 Guide)

Losing a loved one is difficult enough without the added stress of navigating the legal system. If you have been named as the executor of a will — or if you are a family member trying to settle an estate in San Antonio — understanding the probate process is the first step toward resolution. This comprehensive guide walks you through everything you need to know about probating a will in Bexar County, Texas, in 2026.

What Is Probate?

Probate is the legal process through which a deceased person's will is validated by a court, and their estate is distributed to the rightful beneficiaries. In Texas, probate is handled by the county court where the decedent lived at the time of death. For San Antonio residents, that means filing with the Bexar County Probate Court.

The probate process serves several important purposes. First, it confirms that the will presented to the court is the authentic, most recent version of the decedent's wishes. Second, it officially appoints an executor (called an "independent executor" in Texas) who has the legal authority to manage the estate. Third, it provides a structured framework for paying any debts the deceased owed and distributing the remaining assets to the beneficiaries named in the will.

Texas is known for having one of the most streamlined probate systems in the United States, particularly when the will includes a provision for "independent administration." This means that in many cases, the executor can manage the estate without ongoing court supervision, which saves time and money compared to states that require "supervised" probate.

When Is Probate Needed in Texas?

Not every estate requires probate. In Texas, probate is generally necessary when:

  • The deceased owned real property (a house, land, or commercial property) in their name alone, without a transfer-on-death deed or joint ownership with right of survivorship.
  • Bank accounts or financial assets are held solely in the decedent's name without a payable-on-death (POD) or transfer-on-death (TOD) designation.
  • The estate has debts that need to be resolved before assets can be distributed.
  • There are disputes among family members or potential heirs about the distribution of assets.
  • Title companies, banks, or other institutions require court documentation (Letters Testamentary) before releasing assets to the executor.

On the other hand, probate may not be required if all assets pass automatically through beneficiary designations (like life insurance or retirement accounts), joint ownership, or a living trust. Estates valued at $75,000 or less (excluding homestead and exempt property) may qualify for a simplified Small Estate Affidavit instead of full probate.

Step-by-Step Probate Process in San Antonio

Here is the general process for probating a will in Bexar County. While every estate is unique, most cases follow these steps:

Step 1: Locate the Original Will

The original will is essential. Texas courts require the original document — not a copy — to admit a will to probate. Check the decedent's home, safe deposit box, or attorney's office. In Texas, a will must be filed for probate within four years of the date of death. If the original cannot be found, additional legal steps may be necessary to prove the will's contents, which can complicate and delay the process.

Step 2: Hire a Probate Attorney (Recommended)

While Texas law does not strictly require you to hire an attorney to probate a will, it is strongly recommended. A probate attorney can prepare and file all the necessary documents, represent you at the court hearing, and guide you through the executor's duties. Attorney fees for a straightforward, uncontested probate in San Antonio typically range from $1,500 to $4,000, depending on the complexity of the estate.

Step 3: File an Application for Probate

Your attorney will prepare an "Application to Probate Will and for Issuance of Letters Testamentary" and file it with the Bexar County Probate Court. This application includes information about the decedent, the will, the proposed executor, and the known heirs. The court filing fee in Bexar County is approximately $300–$400 as of 2026.

Step 4: Post Notice and Wait

After the application is filed, Texas law requires a waiting period. The court clerk posts a citation at the courthouse for at least 10 days to notify the public that the will has been offered for probate. This gives any interested party the opportunity to contest the will or the appointment of the executor.

Step 5: Attend the Probate Hearing

After the waiting period, the court schedules a hearing. In uncontested cases, this hearing is typically brief — often less than 10 minutes. The executor (or the attorney on their behalf) testifies that the will is valid, that the decedent has passed, and that the proposed executor is qualified to serve. If the judge is satisfied, the will is admitted to probate and the executor is officially appointed.

Step 6: Receive Letters Testamentary

Once the court approves the application, the executor receives "Letters Testamentary." This is the legal document that gives you the authority to act on behalf of the estate — accessing bank accounts, selling property, paying debts, and distributing assets. Banks, title companies, and other institutions will require certified copies of this document.

Step 7: Notify Creditors

Within one month of receiving Letters Testamentary, the executor must publish a notice to creditors in a newspaper of general circulation in Bexar County. This notifies any potential creditors that the estate is in probate and gives them a window to submit claims. The executor must also notify any known creditors individually by certified mail.

Step 8: Inventory the Estate

Within 90 days of being appointed, the executor must file an "Inventory, Appraisement, and List of Claims" with the court. This document lists all the estate's assets, their appraised values, and any outstanding debts or claims. Alternatively, if the will provides for independent administration, the executor may file an affidavit in lieu of the full inventory, keeping the details private.

Step 9: Pay Debts and Distribute Assets

The executor pays any valid debts, taxes, and administrative expenses from the estate's funds. After all obligations are satisfied, the remaining assets are distributed to the beneficiaries according to the terms of the will. The executor should obtain receipts or signed releases from each beneficiary to document the distributions.

Step 10: Close the Estate

In an independent administration, there is no formal requirement to file a closing document with the court. However, it is good practice to prepare a final accounting for the beneficiaries showing all income, expenses, and distributions. This protects the executor from future claims and provides a clear record of the estate's administration.

Types of Probate in Texas

Texas offers several types of probate proceedings, each suited to different situations:

Independent Administration

This is the most common and most efficient type of probate in Texas. If the will names an independent executor (and most well-drafted wills do), the executor can manage the estate with minimal court oversight. After the initial court hearing, the executor handles everything — paying debts, selling property, distributing assets — without needing to go back to court for approval. This is faster and less expensive than dependent administration.

Dependent Administration

If the will does not provide for independent administration, or if the court determines that oversight is necessary, the estate goes through dependent (or "supervised") administration. In this process, the executor must obtain court approval for virtually every action — selling property, paying debts, and making distributions. This process is significantly slower and more expensive due to the repeated court filings and hearings.

Muniment of Title

If the estate has no unpaid debts (other than debts secured by real property), the court may admit the will as a "muniment of title." This is a simplified procedure that validates the will and transfers property to the beneficiaries without appointing an executor. It is faster and cheaper than full administration and works well for straightforward estates where the primary asset is a home.

Small Estate Affidavit

For estates valued at $75,000 or less (excluding homestead and exempt property), Texas allows heirs to file a Small Estate Affidavit instead of going through formal probate. This is the simplest and least expensive option, but it has strict eligibility requirements and is only available when there is no will or when all heirs agree.

How Much Does Probate Cost in San Antonio?

The cost of probate in San Antonio varies depending on the complexity of the estate and the type of probate proceeding. Here is a general breakdown:

  • Court filing fees: $300–$400 in Bexar County
  • Attorney fees (uncontested, independent administration): $1,500–$4,000
  • Attorney fees (contested or dependent administration): $5,000–$15,000+
  • Muniment of Title attorney fees: $1,000–$2,500
  • Newspaper publication notice: $100–$300
  • Certified copies of Letters Testamentary: $2–$5 each
  • Appraisal fees (if real property needs to be valued): $300–$500

Probate Timeline: How Long Does It Take?

The timeline for probate in Texas depends on several factors, including the type of proceeding, the complexity of the estate, and whether any disputes arise. Here are typical timelines:

  • Muniment of Title: 4–8 weeks from filing to completion
  • Independent Administration (uncontested): 6–12 months from filing to final distribution
  • Dependent Administration: 12–24 months or longer
  • Contested Probate: 1–3 years, depending on the nature of the dispute

The mandatory 10-day posting period after filing means that even the simplest probate case takes at least 2–3 weeks from filing to the court hearing. After the hearing, the time needed to complete the administration depends on the estate's assets, debts, and the cooperation of the beneficiaries.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I probate a will without an attorney in Texas?

Technically, yes. Texas law allows individuals to represent themselves in probate court. However, the process involves specific legal documents, court procedures, and deadlines that can be confusing for someone without legal training. A mistake can delay the process by months or result in personal liability for the executor. We strongly recommend consulting with a probate attorney, especially since many offer free initial consultations.

What happens if there is no will?

If someone dies without a will (called "intestate"), their estate is distributed according to Texas intestacy laws. The distribution depends on whether the person was married, had children, or had other living relatives. An "heirship proceeding" may be needed to determine the legal heirs, which can be more complex and expensive than probating a will.

What if the will was not filed within four years?

Texas law requires a will to be filed for probate within four years of the date of death. If this deadline is missed, the will can still be admitted to probate as a "muniment of title," but it cannot be used to appoint an executor. The person seeking to admit the will must provide a reasonable explanation for the delay.

Do all assets go through probate?

No. Assets that pass outside of probate include life insurance proceeds with a named beneficiary, retirement accounts (401k, IRA) with a named beneficiary, bank accounts with payable-on-death designations, property held in joint tenancy with right of survivorship, and assets held in a living trust.

Can a will be contested in Texas?

Yes. A will can be contested on several grounds, including lack of testamentary capacity (the person was not of sound mind when they made the will), undue influence (someone pressured the person into making or changing the will), fraud or forgery, or failure to meet Texas legal requirements for a valid will (such as having two witnesses). Will contests can add significant time and expense to the probate process.
