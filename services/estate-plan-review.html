Keep Your Plan Current

An Outdated Estate Plan Can Be Worse Than No Plan at All

You did the responsible thing. You created an estate plan -- a will, powers of attorney, maybe even a trust. But life does not stand still. You got married or divorced. You had another child. You bought a new home. Your parents passed away. Your executor moved to another state. That agent you named ten years ago? You have not spoken to them in five years.

An estate plan is not a "set it and forget it" document. It needs to keep up with your life. An outdated plan can create confusion, family disputes, and legal complications that are worse than having no plan at all. Imagine your ex-spouse inheriting your retirement account because you forgot to update your beneficiary designations. Or your estranged sibling being named as your medical agent because that is what you wrote down twenty years ago.

Our Estate Plan Review service takes the stress out of keeping your documents current. We review everything you have, identify what needs updating, and make the necessary changes quickly and affordably. Whether your plan was created by us or another attorney, we can help.

When Should You Review Your Estate Plan?

We recommend a review every three to five years as a general rule. But certain life events should trigger an immediate review:

  • Marriage or divorce
  • Birth or adoption of a child or grandchild
  • Death of a spouse, beneficiary, executor, or agent
  • Significant change in assets (buying/selling property, inheritance, retirement)
  • Moving to a new state
  • Change in health (yours or a family member's)
  • Change in relationship with a named agent or beneficiary
  • Changes in tax laws or estate planning laws
Thorough Review

What's Included

A comprehensive review of your entire estate plan with clear recommendations.

Complete Document Review

We read through every document in your existing plan -- will, trust, powers of attorney, deeds, beneficiary designations -- and check them against current Texas law.

Life Changes Assessment

We talk with you about what has changed since your plan was created -- family changes, financial changes, health changes, and relationship changes -- and assess how they affect your plan.

Gap Analysis

We identify what is missing from your plan. Many older plans lack HIPAA authorizations, directives to physicians, or proper beneficiary designations on retirement accounts and insurance policies.

Written Recommendations

You receive a clear, written summary of our findings and recommendations -- what is working, what needs updating, and what new documents you should consider adding.

Updates & Amendments

If changes are needed, we prepare amendments, codicils, or entirely new documents as appropriate. Simple updates are often included in the review fee; more substantial changes are quoted separately at a fair, flat rate.

Beneficiary Designation Check

We review your retirement account, life insurance, and bank account beneficiary designations to make sure they align with your overall estate plan. These designations override your will, so getting them right is critical.

Is This Right for You?

Who Needs an Estate Plan Review?

Anyone Whose Plan Is 3+ Years Old

Laws change. Your life changes. If it has been more than three years since you last looked at your estate plan, it is time for a checkup.

Recently Married or Divorced

A new marriage means new beneficiaries and possibly new agents. A divorce means your ex-spouse may still be named in your documents -- and in Texas, some of those designations may still be legally effective.

New Parents or Grandparents

A new child or grandchild means you may need to update guardian designations, add new beneficiaries, or consider testamentary trusts to protect their inheritance.

After a Death in the Family

If a beneficiary, executor, or named agent has passed away, your plan needs immediate attention to name replacements and avoid gaps in coverage.

Plans Created by Another Attorney

It does not matter who created your original plan. We are happy to review documents prepared by other attorneys and provide honest, unbiased recommendations about what needs updating.

Major Financial Changes

Bought or sold a home? Received an inheritance? Started a business? Retired? Any significant financial change could mean your plan no longer distributes your assets the way you intend.
