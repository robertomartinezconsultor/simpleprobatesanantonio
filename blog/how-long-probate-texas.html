How Long Does Probate Take in Texas? (2026 Update)

One of the most common questions we hear from families dealing with a loved one's estate is: "How long is this going to take?" The answer depends on several factors, including the type of probate, the complexity of the estate, and whether any disputes arise. In this guide, we break down the typical timelines for each type of probate proceeding in Texas and explain what can speed things up — or slow them down.

Timeline Overview

Here is a quick reference table showing typical probate timelines in Texas:

Type of Probate Filing to Hearing Total Duration
Small Estate Affidavit 1–3 weeks 2–6 weeks
Muniment of Title 2–4 weeks 4–8 weeks
Independent Administration (Uncontested) 2–4 weeks 4–9 months
Dependent Administration 2–6 weeks 12–24 months
Contested Probate Varies 1–3+ years

Important: These are typical ranges based on our experience handling probate cases in Bexar County. Your case may be faster or slower depending on its specific circumstances.

Muniment of Title Timeline

Muniment of title is the fastest form of probate in Texas when applicable. It is available when the estate has no unpaid debts (other than debts secured by real property like a mortgage) and there is a valid will. In this proceeding, the court simply validates the will and uses it as a legal document to transfer title of property to the beneficiaries. No executor is formally appointed, and no ongoing administration is required.

Typical timeline:

  • File the application: Day 1
  • Mandatory posting period: 10+ days
  • Court hearing: 2–4 weeks after filing
  • Record the order with the county clerk: same week as hearing
  • Total: 4–8 weeks

Muniment of title is ideal for estates where the main asset is a house and there are no significant debts. Many of our clients in San Antonio qualify for this streamlined process.

Independent Administration Timeline

Independent administration is the most common type of probate in Texas. The will names an independent executor who, once appointed by the court, can manage the estate without further court oversight. The timeline has two phases: the court phase and the administration phase.

Court phase (2–4 weeks):

  • File the application and original will
  • Wait for the 10-day posting period
  • Attend the court hearing (typically 5–10 minutes)
  • Receive Letters Testamentary

Administration phase (3–8 months):

  • Publish notice to creditors in a local newspaper (within 30 days of appointment)
  • File the estate inventory or affidavit in lieu of inventory (within 90 days)
  • Collect assets, pay debts, and resolve any claims
  • Distribute remaining assets to beneficiaries
  • Prepare final accounting

Total typical timeline: 4–9 months. Simple estates with few assets and cooperative beneficiaries can be completed toward the shorter end. More complex estates with multiple properties, business interests, or tax considerations may take longer.

Dependent Administration Timeline

Dependent (or supervised) administration requires the executor to obtain court approval for nearly every action, including paying debts, selling property, and making distributions. This significantly extends the timeline because each action requires filing a motion, waiting for a hearing, and obtaining a court order.

Typical timeline: 12–24 months. Some dependent administrations can stretch even longer if the estate is complex or if the court's calendar is crowded. Each motion and hearing can add weeks or months to the process.

Dependent administration is usually required when the will does not include a provision for independent administration, when the court determines that oversight is necessary to protect the heirs, or when all heirs agree to request court supervision.

Contested Probate Timeline

When someone contests a will or objects to the executor's appointment, the timeline extends dramatically. Contested probate involves many of the same procedures as a civil lawsuit: discovery, depositions, mediation, and potentially a trial.

Typical timeline: 1–3+ years. The duration depends on the nature and complexity of the dispute, the willingness of the parties to negotiate, and the court's availability. Some contested cases settle through mediation within a few months, while others proceed to trial and even appeal.

Common reasons for contested probate include challenges to the will's validity, disputes between family members about asset distribution, allegations of executor misconduct, and creditor claims that exceed the estate's assets.

Small Estate Affidavit Timeline

The Small Estate Affidavit is the quickest way to transfer assets in Texas, but it is only available for estates valued at $75,000 or less (excluding homestead and exempt property). All heirs must agree, and it can be used regardless of whether there is a will.

Typical timeline: 2–6 weeks. The affidavit is filed with the court, and if approved, it serves as proof of the heirs' right to the decedent's assets. No executor is appointed, and no formal administration is required.

Factors That Affect Probate Duration

Several factors can extend or shorten the probate timeline:

Factors That Slow Things Down

  • Missing or lost original will. If the original will cannot be found, additional legal proceedings are needed to prove its contents, adding weeks or months.
  • Heir disputes. Any disagreement among heirs — even minor ones — can delay distributions and require mediation or litigation.
  • Complex assets. Estates with business interests, out-of-state property, mineral rights, or significant investments take longer to inventory and value.
  • Creditor claims. If creditors file claims against the estate, the executor must evaluate and resolve each one, which can take months.
  • Tax issues. Estates that owe federal estate tax (for estates exceeding $13.61 million in 2026) or have complex income tax situations require additional time for tax preparation and IRS processing.
  • Court backlogs. Bexar County probate courts handle a high volume of cases, and scheduling delays can occur, particularly during busy periods.
  • Executor delays. An executor who is unresponsive, disorganized, or located far from San Antonio can slow the entire process.

Factors That Speed Things Up

  • A well-drafted will with independent administration. This eliminates the need for ongoing court supervision.
  • A self-proving affidavit. Eliminates the need for witness testimony at the hearing.
  • Cooperative beneficiaries. When all heirs agree, the process moves much faster.
  • Simple estate composition. Cash accounts, a single home, and no significant debts make for a quick administration.
  • Experienced probate attorney. An attorney who handles Bexar County probate regularly knows the court's procedures and can avoid common pitfalls.
  • Organized documents. Having the original will, death certificate, asset information, and debt records ready when you first meet with your attorney saves time.

How to Speed Up the Probate Process

If you want to get through probate as quickly as possible, here are our top recommendations:

  1. Hire a local probate attorney immediately. The sooner you engage an attorney, the sooner the process begins. Delays in hiring legal help are the most common reason probate takes longer than necessary.
  2. Gather documents early. Before your first meeting, collect the original will, death certificate (multiple certified copies), a list of known assets and debts, and recent bank and investment statements.
  3. Choose the right type of proceeding. Your attorney can help you determine whether muniment of title, independent administration, or another option is the fastest path for your situation.
  4. Respond to requests promptly. When your attorney or the court needs information, provide it as quickly as possible. Delays in providing requested documents are a common bottleneck.
  5. Communicate with heirs. Keeping all beneficiaries informed reduces the likelihood of disputes and delays.
  6. Consider settling disputes outside of court. If a disagreement arises, mediation is often faster and less expensive than litigation.
