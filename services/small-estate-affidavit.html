Small Estate Affidavit

Home  /  Services  /  Small Estate Affidavit

Skip the Courtroom

A Faster, Simpler Way to Settle a Small Estate

Not every estate needs to go through the full probate process. If your loved one passed away and the total value of their estate is under $75,000 (not counting their homestead and any exempt property), Texas law offers a much simpler alternative: the Small Estate Affidavit.

Instead of filing a full probate case, the heirs sign a sworn affidavit that lists the assets, the debts, and who is entitled to inherit. This affidavit is filed with the probate court, and once approved, it can be used to transfer bank accounts, vehicles, and even real property — all without appointing an executor or administrator.

This is often the right choice for families where a parent or spouse passed away leaving a modest estate — perhaps a small bank account, a vehicle, and personal belongings. It's the most affordable option available, and it can be completed in a matter of weeks rather than months.

We know that dealing with legal paperwork after a loss is the last thing you want to worry about. That's why we handle the preparation, review, and filing for you. You tell us about the estate, and we take care of the rest.

Important note: The $75,000 limit excludes the homestead and certain exempt property. This means that even if your loved one owned a home, you may still qualify — as long as the other assets total less than $75,000. We'll help you determine if this is the right path during your free consultation.

Is This Right for You?

Who Can Use a Small Estate Affidavit?

Estate Under $75,000

The total value of all assets (excluding the homestead and exempt property) is less than $75,000. This includes bank accounts, vehicles, and personal property.

30+ Days Since Passing

At least 30 days have passed since your loved one's death and no one else has filed for probate during that time.

No Will — Or Will Not Filed

This option works when there's no will. If there is a will, it typically must be filed for probate through a different process (like Muniment of Title).

Assets Exceed Debts

The value of the estate's assets (excluding homestead and exempt property) must be enough to cover all known debts and obligations.
