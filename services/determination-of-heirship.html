No Will? We Can Help.

When There's No Will, the Court Determines Who Inherits

Losing someone is hard enough. When that person didn't leave a will, it can feel even more overwhelming. You might be wondering: who gets what? How do we transfer the house? What happens to the bank accounts? The answer, in most cases, is a Determination of Heirship.

This is a court proceeding where a judge officially determines who the legal heirs are under Texas law. When someone dies "intestate" (without a will), Texas has a set of rules — called intestacy laws — that dictate who inherits. The Determination of Heirship proceeding applies those rules to your specific family situation and produces a court order that banks, title companies, and other institutions will rely on.

Unlike an Affidavit of Heirship (which is a simpler, non-court document), a Determination of Heirship produces a binding court judgment. This makes it the gold standard for establishing who inherits. It's required when you need to sell real estate through a title company, when the estate is complex, or when there's any question about who the heirs might be.

The court will also appoint an attorney ad litem — an independent attorney whose job is to investigate and make sure all heirs have been identified. This protects everyone involved and gives the court confidence that the right people are receiving their inheritance.

We know this process can seem intimidating, but it doesn't have to be. We handle the preparation, filing, hearing, and coordination with the attorney ad litem. Your family provides the information about your loved one, and we take care of the rest.

Is This Right for You?

Who Needs a Determination of Heirship?

No Will Was Left

Your loved one passed away without a valid will, and there are assets — especially real estate — that need to be transferred to the rightful heirs.

Need a Court Order

A title company, bank, or other institution is requiring a court order to transfer assets. An Affidavit of Heirship alone isn't sufficient for your situation.

Complex Family Situation

There are multiple marriages, children from different relationships, or questions about paternity that make it important to have a judge make an official determination.

Planning to Sell Real Estate

You need to sell inherited property and the title company requires a court-issued determination of who the legal heirs are before they'll close the transaction.

Step by Step

How the Process Works

We manage the entire process from filing to the final court order.

1

Application & Filing

We prepare and file the application with the Bexar County Probate Court, identifying the deceased, their assets, and all potential heirs.

2

Attorney Ad Litem

The court appoints an independent attorney to investigate and verify the family relationships. We coordinate with them to provide the needed information.

3

Hearing & Court Order

At the hearing, the judge reviews the evidence and issues a binding order declaring who the legal heirs are and their respective shares of the estate.

Once the court order is signed, it can be used to transfer property, close accounts, and distribute assets. If an administrator is needed, the court can appoint one at the same time.

Common Questions

Determination of Heirship FAQ

Texas intestacy laws determine who inherits based on the family situation. If the deceased was married, the surviving spouse typically inherits most or all of the estate, depending on whether there were children from other relationships. If the deceased was unmarried, the estate passes to children, then parents, then siblings, and so on. The specifics depend on your family's unique circumstances — we can explain exactly how it applies to you during a free consultation.
The process typically takes 2-4 months from filing to the final court order. The timeline depends on court scheduling, how quickly the attorney ad litem can complete their investigation, and whether any complications arise. In straightforward cases with clear family relationships, it can sometimes move faster.
An attorney ad litem is an independent lawyer appointed by the court to investigate the family history and make sure all legal heirs have been identified and notified. Their fee is set by the judge and typically ranges from $500 to $1,500, depending on the complexity of the case. This fee is paid by the applicant (or from the estate) and is separate from our flat fee.
An Affidavit of Heirship is a sworn document filed in the county records — it's simpler and cheaper, but it's not a court order. A Determination of Heirship produces a binding court judgment. Title companies, banks, and other institutions almost always accept a court order without question, while acceptance of an affidavit can vary. If you need certainty — especially for a property sale — a Determination of Heirship is the stronger option.
Unlike probating a will (which generally must be done within four years), there is no time limit for filing a Determination of Heirship. You can file it months, years, or even decades after your loved one's passing. This makes it an excellent tool for resolving long-standing property title issues.
