Affidavit of Heirship

Home  /  Services  /  Affidavit of Heirship

No Court Required

Transfer Property Without Going to Court

When a loved one passes away, one of the most common concerns is: "How do we get the house into our name?" If there was no will and the estate is relatively straightforward, an Affidavit of Heirship may be the simplest and most affordable answer.

An Affidavit of Heirship is a sworn document that identifies who the legal heirs are and establishes their right to inherit property — without ever stepping into a courtroom. It's particularly useful for transferring real estate, though it can also help with vehicles, bank accounts, and other assets depending on the institution's requirements.

Here's how it works: a person who knew the deceased and their family — but is not an heir — signs a sworn affidavit describing the family relationships, confirming there was no will, and identifying all the legal heirs. This affidavit is then recorded in the county deed records where the property is located, effectively establishing a chain of title.

This is a tool that families in San Antonio have used for generations to pass property from one generation to the next. It's especially common in situations where a parent passes away and the children need to sell the family home or refinance a mortgage.

While an Affidavit of Heirship is not a court order — and title companies may occasionally require additional documentation — it is widely accepted throughout Texas and is often the fastest path to resolving property ownership questions after a death.

Your loved one passed away without a will, and you need to establish who the legal heirs are for property transfer purposes.

Real Estate to Transfer

There's a house, land, or other real property that needs to be transferred into the heirs' names — whether to keep, sell, or refinance.

Want to Avoid Court

You'd prefer to handle the property transfer without the time and expense of a full probate court proceeding.

Clear Family Situation

The family relationships are straightforward and there are no disputes about who should inherit. All heirs are in agreement.

How It Works

No courtroom. No judge. Just a properly prepared legal document.

Gather Information

We collect details about the deceased, their family members, the property, and identify a qualified person to serve as the affiant (witness).

2

Prepare & Sign

We draft the affidavit with all the legally required information. The affiant and witnesses sign before a notary public.

3

Record the Document

We file the affidavit in the county deed records. After recording, it becomes part of the property's chain of title — establishing the heirs' ownership.

There is no time limit for filing an Affidavit of Heirship. In fact, this tool is often used to clear up title issues from deaths that occurred years or even decades ago.
