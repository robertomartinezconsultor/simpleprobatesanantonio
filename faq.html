General Probate

Probate costs in San Antonio typically range from $1,000 to $5,000 in attorney fees, depending on the complexity of the estate and the type of probate required. Court filing fees in Bexar County run approximately $300 to $400, and newspaper publication costs add another $100 to $200. At Joe's Probate Services, we offer flat-fee pricing so you know your exact cost upfront -- a muniment of title starts at $2,500 and a small estate affidavit starts at $1,500. We'll provide a complete cost breakdown during your free consultation.
A straightforward probate case in Bexar County typically takes 3 to 6 months from filing to completion. A muniment of title, the simplest form of probate in Texas, can often be completed in as little as 6 to 8 weeks. Independent administration cases usually take 4 to 6 months. Timelines can vary based on court schedules, the complexity of assets, and whether any issues arise during the process. Our local knowledge of the Bexar County courthouse helps us move cases efficiently.
While Texas law does not strictly require an attorney for all probate matters, it is strongly recommended. Probate involves court filings, legal deadlines, and procedural requirements that can be difficult to navigate without legal training. Mistakes can cause significant delays, added expense, and even the denial of your application. Most Bexar County judges expect represented parties, and having an experienced probate attorney ensures your case moves through the system efficiently and correctly.
To begin probate in Texas, you'll typically need the original will (if one exists), a certified death certificate, information about the deceased's assets and debts, and identification for the person applying to be executor or administrator. Additional helpful documents include property deeds, bank and investment account statements, vehicle titles, and life insurance policies. During your free consultation, we'll provide a complete, personalized checklist so you know exactly what to gather.
Some probate alternatives in Texas can minimize or avoid full court proceedings. A small estate affidavit may be used for estates valued under $75,000 (excluding homestead and exempt property) and requires only a court-approved affidavit rather than full administration. An affidavit of heirship can transfer real property without any court involvement at all. However, most probate cases require at least one court hearing. The good news is that we attend the hearing on your behalf, so the process is typically quick and straightforward.
Probate is the court process of validating a will and authorizing someone (the executor or administrator) to act on behalf of the estate. Estate administration is the broader process of managing and distributing the deceased person's assets, paying debts, filing final tax returns, and closing accounts. Probate is one step within estate administration. In Texas, some estates require full court-supervised administration, while others can use simplified methods like muniment of title or small estate affidavits.
When a house goes through probate in Texas, the court determines the rightful heir or beneficiary based on the will or intestacy laws. The homestead property receives special protections under Texas law -- it's exempt from most creditor claims and cannot be forced to be sold to pay the deceased person's debts. Once probate is complete, the property is transferred to the heir through a court order, deed, or muniment of title. The heir can then choose to keep, sell, or refinance the property as they see fit.
In Texas, a will should ideally be filed for probate within four years of the date of death. After four years, you can still file, but the court can only admit the will as a muniment of title -- not for full administration. You must also prove you were not at fault for the delay in filing. If more than four years have passed and you cannot meet these requirements, you may need to pursue alternative methods such as a determination of heirship or affidavit of heirship to transfer property. Probate Types

A muniment of title is the simplest and fastest form of probate available in Texas. It allows the court to admit a will to probate and use it as a direct transfer document for assets -- without appointing an executor or requiring ongoing court supervision. You may qualify if the deceased left a valid will, there are no unpaid debts (other than debts secured by real estate), and no formal estate administration is needed. It's our most recommended probate option and typically the most affordable, starting at $2,500.
A small estate affidavit is a simplified probate alternative available when someone dies without a will and the estate's value (excluding homestead and exempt property) does not exceed $75,000. All heirs must agree and sign the affidavit, which lists the deceased's assets, debts, and legal heirs. The affidavit must be approved by the probate court. It avoids the need for full administration, making it faster and more affordable. Our flat fee for small estate affidavits starts at $1,500.
When someone dies without a will (called dying "intestate") in Texas, state law determines who inherits the estate based on family relationships. The distribution depends on whether the deceased was married, had children, or had other surviving relatives. For example, if the deceased was married with children, the surviving spouse typically inherits the homestead and community property, while separate property may be divided between the spouse and children. The process usually requires a determination of heirship or small estate affidavit, both of which we handle regularly.
Independent administration allows the executor to manage the estate with minimal court supervision -- they can sell property, pay debts, and distribute assets without court approval for each action. This is faster, cheaper, and the standard in Texas. Dependent administration, by contrast, requires court approval for virtually every action, making it significantly slower and more expensive. Texas strongly favors independent administration, and most wills specifically grant independent authority. When there's no will, all heirs can agree to independent administration to avoid the dependent route.
An affidavit of heirship is a sworn legal document that identifies the heirs of a deceased person and can be used to transfer real property without going through probate court. It must include testimony from a disinterested witness -- someone who knew the deceased and their family but doesn't stand to inherit. The completed affidavit is filed in the county deed records where the property is located. Most title companies require the affidavit to be on record for five years before they'll insure the title without additional requirements. Our flat fee for preparing an affidavit of heirship starts at $1,000.

Estate Planning

A will allows you to control who inherits your property, name a guardian for minor children, and appoint a trusted executor to manage your estate. Without a will, Texas intestacy laws dictate how your property is distributed, which may not align with your wishes at all. Having a properly drafted will with a self-proving affidavit also makes probate significantly faster and less expensive for your family. Our Essential estate plan, which includes a simple will and self-proving affidavit, is available for just $500.
A revocable living trust is an estate planning tool that allows you to transfer assets into a trust during your lifetime while maintaining full control as the trustee. You can modify or revoke the trust at any time. When you pass away, the assets in the trust transfer directly to your beneficiaries without going through probate, which provides privacy (since probate is public record), avoids delays and court costs, and can be especially beneficial for families with property in multiple states. Our Premium estate plan includes a revocable living trust for $2,500.
A transfer on death deed (TODD) is a Texas legal document that designates a beneficiary who will automatically receive your real property when you die, completely bypassing probate. You retain full ownership and control during your lifetime and can revoke or change the deed at any time. It's one of the most affordable ways to keep a specific property out of probate. The deed must be properly executed, notarized, and filed in the county deed records before the owner's death to be valid. We prepare TOD deeds for $500.
A Lady Bird deed (also called an enhanced life estate deed) allows a property owner to transfer real estate to a beneficiary upon death while retaining full control during their lifetime. Unlike a regular life estate deed, the owner can sell, mortgage, or lease the property without the beneficiary's permission. It avoids probate for that specific property and may help preserve Medicaid eligibility, making it a popular estate planning tool for Texas homeowners. We prepare Lady Bird deeds for a flat fee of $500.
A comprehensive Texas estate plan should include a last will and testament, a durable power of attorney for financial matters, a medical power of attorney, a HIPAA authorization, and a directive to physicians (living will). Depending on your circumstances, you may also benefit from a revocable living trust, transfer on death deeds, Lady Bird deeds, and updated beneficiary designations on financial accounts and life insurance. Our Complete estate plan package at $1,200 covers all the essential documents most families need.
You should review your estate plan every 3 to 5 years, or whenever a major life event occurs such as marriage, divorce, the birth of a child, the death of a named beneficiary or executor, a significant change in your financial situation, or a move to a new state. An outdated estate plan can be worse than no plan at all -- it may direct your assets to the wrong people or fail to account for your current wishes. We offer estate plan reviews for $300 to make sure your documents are current and effective.
A power of attorney is a legal document that authorizes someone you trust (called your "agent") to make decisions on your behalf. A durable power of attorney for financial matters allows your agent to manage finances, pay bills, and handle business transactions if you become incapacitated. A medical power of attorney designates someone to make healthcare decisions when you cannot communicate them yourself. Both are critical components of any estate plan and can prevent the need for costly and time-consuming guardianship proceedings. Standalone powers of attorney are available for $400.
