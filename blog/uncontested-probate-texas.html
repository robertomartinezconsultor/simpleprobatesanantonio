What Is Uncontested Probate and Do You Qualify? (Texas Guide 2026)

When a loved one passes away and leaves a will, one of the first questions families ask is: "How complicated is this going to be?" The good news is that the majority of probate cases in Texas are uncontested, meaning the process is relatively straightforward, affordable, and quick. In this guide, we explain exactly what uncontested probate means, how it differs from contested probate, whether your case qualifies, and what the process looks like step by step.

What Is Uncontested Probate?

Uncontested probate means that no one disputes or challenges the validity of the will, the appointment of the executor, or the distribution of the estate's assets. All heirs and beneficiaries agree with the terms of the will and the actions of the executor, allowing the process to move forward smoothly without litigation.

In Texas, the vast majority of probate cases — estimated at over 90% — are uncontested. This is partly because Texas law favors independent administration, which gives executors broad authority to manage the estate without constant court supervision. When a case is uncontested, it typically involves a single court hearing, minimal paperwork beyond the initial filing, and a much shorter timeline compared to contested cases.

Key Takeaway: If everyone agrees with the will and the executor, your probate case is uncontested. This means faster resolution, lower costs, and less stress for the family.

Uncontested vs. Contested Probate

Understanding the difference between uncontested and contested probate helps you know what to expect:

Uncontested Probate occurs when all interested parties (heirs, beneficiaries, and creditors) accept the will as valid and agree with the executor's appointment. The court hearing is brief, usually lasting 5–10 minutes. The executor receives Letters Testamentary and proceeds to administer the estate independently. There is no need for trial, mediation, or extended litigation.

Contested Probate occurs when one or more parties challenge some aspect of the will or the estate administration. Common grounds for contesting include claims that the decedent lacked mental capacity when making the will, allegations of undue influence by a caregiver or family member, questions about whether the will was properly executed under Texas law, disputes over the interpretation of the will's terms, or objections to the proposed executor's qualifications.

Contested probate can drag on for months or even years, require depositions and expert witnesses, and cost tens of thousands of dollars in legal fees. By contrast, uncontested probate is measured in weeks and costs a fraction of the price.

Do You Qualify for Uncontested Probate?

Your probate case is likely to be uncontested if the following conditions are met:

  • A valid will exists. The decedent left a will that meets Texas legal requirements (signed by the testator, witnessed by two people over 14 years old, or a holographic will entirely in the decedent's handwriting).
  • All heirs and beneficiaries agree. No one is challenging the will's validity or the way assets are distributed.
  • The executor is willing and qualified. The person named as executor in the will is willing to serve, is not disqualified under Texas law (e.g., not a convicted felon), and no one objects to their appointment.
  • The will was filed within four years. Texas requires the will to be submitted for probate within four years of the date of death.
  • The original will is available. Texas courts require the original document, not a copy.
  • No creditor disputes. No creditors are challenging the executor's handling of the estate's debts.

If any of these conditions are not met, your case may still proceed through probate, but additional steps or hearings may be required. Even if one heir initially objects, many cases are resolved through negotiation or mediation before they escalate to a full contested proceeding.

Benefits of Uncontested Probate

Choosing uncontested probate (or ensuring your case stays uncontested) offers significant advantages:

  • Lower cost. Attorney fees for uncontested probate in San Antonio typically range from $1,500 to $3,500, compared to $5,000–$15,000+ for contested cases.
  • Faster resolution. Most uncontested cases are completed within 3–6 months, while contested cases can take 1–3 years.
  • Less stress. Avoiding courtroom battles preserves family relationships and reduces emotional strain during an already difficult time.
  • Privacy. In independent administration, the executor may file an affidavit in lieu of a public inventory, keeping the estate's financial details private.
  • Simplicity. The executor can manage the estate without repeated trips to the courthouse for approvals.

The Uncontested Probate Process in Texas

Here is what the uncontested probate process looks like in Bexar County:

  1. Consult a probate attorney. Discuss your situation and gather the necessary documents, including the original will, the death certificate, and a list of the decedent's assets and debts.
  2. File the application. Your attorney files an Application to Probate Will and for Issuance of Letters Testamentary with the Bexar County Probate Court, along with the applicable filing fee.
  3. Mandatory waiting period. A citation is posted at the courthouse for at least 10 days, giving interested parties the opportunity to object.
  4. Court hearing. After the waiting period, you (or your attorney) attend a brief hearing. The judge confirms the will is valid, appoints the executor, and issues Letters Testamentary.
  5. Administer the estate. The executor collects assets, publishes a notice to creditors, pays debts and taxes, and distributes the remaining assets to the beneficiaries.
  6. Close the estate. Once all debts are paid and assets distributed, the executor prepares a final accounting and the administration is complete.

Tips to Keep Probate Uncontested

If you are planning your own estate or serving as an executor, here are practical steps to minimize the risk of a contested probate:

  • Work with an attorney to draft your will. A properly drafted will that clearly expresses your wishes and meets all Texas legal requirements is much harder to challenge.
  • Communicate with your family. Letting your heirs know your intentions in advance can prevent surprises and resentment later.
  • Choose the right executor. Select someone who is trustworthy, organized, and unlikely to create conflict with the beneficiaries.
  • Keep your will updated. Major life events (marriage, divorce, birth of a child, significant asset changes) should trigger a review of your will.
  • Store your original will safely. Make sure your executor knows where to find it. Consider leaving it with your attorney or in a fireproof safe.
  • Consider a self-proving affidavit. In Texas, a will with a self-proving affidavit (signed by the testator and witnesses before a notary) can be admitted to probate without requiring the witnesses to testify in court, making the process even smoother.
