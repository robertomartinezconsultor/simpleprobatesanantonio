You're Not Alone in This

Step-by-Step Support for the Responsibility Ahead

Being named as an executor or administrator is an honor — it means someone trusted you to take care of their final affairs. But it's also a significant responsibility, and most people have never done it before. It's completely normal to feel uncertain about what you're supposed to do, when you're supposed to do it, and how to avoid making costly mistakes.

That's exactly why we created the Executor/Administrator Guidance Package. This service is designed for people who have already been appointed by the court (or are about to be) and need a clear, organized roadmap for fulfilling their duties.

We sit down with you and walk through everything — step by step. What needs to happen first? What are the deadlines? How do you notify creditors? When can you distribute assets? What records do you need to keep? How do you protect yourself from personal liability?

You'll leave our meeting with a personalized action plan — a written checklist customized to your specific estate, with clear timelines and practical instructions. Think of it as your personal GPS for navigating the executor role from start to finish.

This service is perfect for executors who want to handle the administration themselves but need the confidence that comes from professional guidance. And if you run into questions along the way, follow-up consultations are available at an affordable hourly rate.

Common Questions

Executor Guidance FAQ

In Texas, an independent executor's main duties include: securing and inventorying estate assets, notifying known creditors, publishing a notice to creditors in a local newspaper, paying valid debts and taxes, filing the Inventory with the court (within 90 days), maintaining accurate records of all transactions, and ultimately distributing the remaining assets to beneficiaries according to the will. Our guidance package walks you through each of these responsibilities in detail.

Our guidance package specifically addresses the most common mistakes executors make and how to avoid them.
The timeline varies significantly depending on the estate's complexity. Simple estates with few assets and no disputes can sometimes be settled in 3-6 months. More complex estates with real property to sell, creditor claims to resolve, or tax returns to file may take 12-18 months or longer. Our guidance package includes a realistic timeline estimate based on your specific estate so you know what to expect.
Texas law allows executors to receive "reasonable compensation" for their services, typically up to 5% of the gross estate value. However, the will may specify a different amount (or no compensation at all). Any compensation is taxable income to the executor. We'll review what the will says and advise you on what's appropriate for your situation.
Beneficiary disputes are one of the most stressful parts of being an executor. The best defense is transparency and proper documentation. Our guidance package includes advice on communicating with beneficiaries, keeping records that protect you, and when to seek additional legal help.
